Clock correction

Most cheap sound cards have inaccurate clocks, that lead to a bias in the frequencies detected and thus biased values in the Offset box (with biases up to a few cents). The clock correction factor is a number that multiplies the detected frequency, thus cancelling the bias.

You can easily calibrate NOOT for your sound card by using a tuning fork (or an equivalent instrument). The clock correction factor in cents is the opposite of the Offset value shown when the tuning fork is struck near the microphone.

The right sampling rate

With the default algorithm (Continuous power spectrum), the best sampling rate is the lowest, provided that it's at least two times the maximum frequency to be measured. Usually 8000 or 16000 Hz will go.

With the autocorrelation-based algorithm, the best sampling rate is the highest. However, if the note isn't detected correctly, or if you see glitches, you can consider lowering it, taking into account that lower sampling rates cause longer reaction times.

Tuning extremely high or low notes

Extreme notes are those below 100 Hz and above 3000 Hz. These are not easily detected by the program. In order to make the tuner work well with these notes, you should first set the Note and Octave options in the main window, to prevent NOOT from detecting a wrong note. Then, you should raise the Expected precision option. NOOT cannot always satisfy the precision constraint with extreme notes.
